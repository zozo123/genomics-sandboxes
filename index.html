01

Why it's personal

In March 2025, 23andMe filed for bankruptcy. The DNA of 15 million people became an asset for sale; twenty-seven state attorneys general sued to stop it; traffic to the delete-your-data page jumped 526% in a day. The lesson the self-genomics crowd took from it was simple: don't park your genome in a company's permanent database.

  1. You can just read it yourself. A 23andMe export, a Nebula or Nucleus whole-genome file, the public reference — it's all just text: four letters, a few billion times. The barrier was never the data. It was the machine.
  2. The interesting signal is regulatory, not spelling. Most consumer genomics stops at variants. But what your cells do with the same DNA is set by methylation at 3,201,954 CpG sites — the switches behind biological-age clocks, tumor screens, and cell identity. That's what this demo maps.
  3. A sandbox is a computer that dies on purpose. Analyze your genome on a box that's disposable by design: it boots, reads, reports, and vanishes. Your most personal data never outlives the question you asked of it. That's the opposite of a database that outlives the company.

the moveBring your own genome. Shard it, fork a warm reference once, scan, reduce — then throw the computers away.

02

The pattern: map-reduce, with a warm broadcast

MapReduce's real trick was never "go parallel." It was move the work to where the data already lives, and broadcast the read-only base once. In genomics the read-only base is huge and slow to stage: reference + index + toolchain. So you pay for it once — and freeze it.

  1. Warm one boxInstall the toolchain, download the reference chromosomes, build the index. The slow part — paid once.
  2. Snapshot itFreeze the warm box to a 127 MB image in ~4s. This is the MapReduce broadcast: the read-only base, ready to hand to every worker.
  3. Fork per chromosomeEach shard restores the snapshot — warm, identical, instant. No re-downloading the genome. These are the mappers.
  4. ReduceMerge the per-shard JSON into one genome-wide landscape. Then delete every box.

why a snapshot and not a container imageA container image is the cold start: every worker pulls and re-provisions. A VM snapshot is the warm start — memory and disk captured at an instant, restored copy-on-write. Same idea as Firecracker microVM snapshots or Lambda SnapStart: skip the boot, skip the staging, fork the finished state.

03

The run — and a free correctness check

Five human chromosomes from GRCh38 — chr19, chr20, chr21, chr22, chrY — 278 Mb in all. Each shard computes GC% and CpG observed/expected in 1 Mb bins, and counts CpG-island candidates (Gardiner-Garden & Frommer: 200 bp window, GC > 50%, obs/exp > 0.6).

04

What the snapshot actually buys

The naive way: every worker stages the reference itself. The snapshot way: stage once, fork. The first run is faster; the real payoff is every run after — and the redundant work you never do.

Swap the 72 MB of test chromosomes for a real 3 GB reference plus BWA indices, fan out across a whole cohort, and the snapshot stops being an optimization and becomes the only sane way to do it. Every fork also starts byte-identical — which is reproducibility, the thing genomics actually grades you on.

05

Reproduce it

The whole demo is four verbs of the islo CLI. No Kubernetes, no Nextflow, no cluster account.

# 1 · warm base: toolchain + GRCh38 chromosomes + index (paid once)
islo use gx-warm -- bash -lc './warmup.sh chr19 chr20 chr21 chr22 chrY'

# 2 · broadcast: freeze the warm box to a snapshot
islo snapshot save gx-warm --name genomics-warm

# 3 · MAP: fork one warm box per chromosome, in parallel
for chr in chr19 chr20 chr21 chr22 chrY; do
  islo use gx-map-$chr --snapshot genomics-warm -- \
    python3 compute.py $chr &        # each starts warm, just computes
done; wait

# 4 · REDUCE: merge per-shard JSON → genome-wide landscape, then delete the boxes
python3 reduce.py *.json > landscape.json

⌗

Objections

"This is GC content, not medicine." Correct — and on purpose. It's a transparent, verifiable proxy that exercises the exact pattern real pipelines use (scatter by interval, gather results). Swap the kernel for DeepVariant or a methylation caller; the map-reduce-on-snapshots shape doesn't change.

"Your island calls are loose." They are candidates. The Gardiner-Garden & Frommer 1987 rule (200 bp, GC > 50%, obs/exp > 0.6) over-calls GC-rich repeats; Takai & Jones (2002) tightened it to 500 bp / GC > 55% / obs/exp > 0.65. The point here is the shape and gradient across chromosomes, which both rules agree on — not a curated annotation like ENCODE's cCRE Registry (Moore et al., Nature 2020).

"Chromosomes aren't balanced shards." True — chrY is mostly gaps, chr20 is the biggest here. The warm fork wall-clock is set by the slowest shard, which is why real pipelines shard by fixed intervals. The snapshot argument is orthogonal to how you cut the work.

"Is forking a snapshot really reproducible?" More than re-provisioning is. Every shard restores the same captured disk+memory state, copy-on-write — not "the same Dockerfile, fingers crossed the mirrors agree today."

"Privacy theater?" The box is ephemeral and isolated, and it's yours for its lifetime. Your genome is analyzed in a computer you control and then destroy — not deposited into a third party's permanent, sellable database.
